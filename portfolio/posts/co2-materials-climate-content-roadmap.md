# CO2, materials and thermal evidence: planning the publication ladder

I am working on a research project around **CO2 metrics, surface materials, solar absorption and thermal evidence**. The next step is not only to keep collecting data, but to design a responsible way to communicate the results.

The core idea is simple:

> Global climate metrics and models are useful, but they can hide local, temporal and material dynamics that are directly measurable. The project aims to recover experimental evidence —including thermal images and field data— to compare hypotheses, limits and practical consequences.

This is not a campaign to turn one thermal photograph into a universal climate conclusion. It is a research and communication pipeline: measure carefully, document metadata, separate local observations from global claims, and only scale the message when the evidence supports it.

## The research angles

The project has several connected tracks:

| Track | Main question | Evidence needed | Main risk if overstated |
|---|---|---|---|
| CO2 and metrics | What do metrics such as GWP/GTP hide when they summarise complex processes? | IPCC/AR6 sources, GWP* literature, response models | Turning metric criticism into global denial |
| Local CO2 depletion | How does CO2 behave in controlled or biologically active systems? | NCER simulations, greenhouse literature, parameter sensitivity | Extrapolating local dynamics to the global atmosphere too quickly |
| Materials and solar absorption | How much do surface temperatures differ between materials under the same sun? | Thermal sessions, CSV files, normal/thermal photos, controls | Treating a pilot as a universal law |
| Urban materials | What do asphalt, tiles, concrete, vegetation, PV panels or roofs imply locally? | Repeated measurements, rankings, albedo literature | Ignoring humidity, ventilation, mass and context |
| Critical climate methodology | Where are aggregated assumptions misused or badly communicated? | Primary sources and careful distinction between scientific reports and regulatory use | Sounding ideological rather than technical |
| Commercial application | What can companies or cities decide from local thermal audits? | Protocols, before/after evidence, recommendations and limits | Selling diagnosis before validating the method |

## The content ladder

The plan is to move from small, low-risk publications to larger and more consequential formats.

### 1. Internal notes

First, short dated notes: one idea, one evidence source, one limitation and one possible visual. Examples:

- why `Delta_T_white` is more honest than jumping to CO2e in a thermal pilot;
- why emissivity can ruin an attractive thermal comparison;
- why GWP100 is not local temperature.

### 2. X/Twitter posts and threads

Small pieces can show progress without pretending that the research is finished:

- one thermal image plus one caveat;
- a 5–8 post technical thread;
- a short explanation of a metric such as GWP, GTP or GWP*.

Every public post should answer: what was measured, when, under which conditions, and what cannot be concluded yet.

### 3. Short videos

Thermal images are visual, so they can become 30–90 second videos:

- “We are not measuring CO2e: we are measuring surface heat.”
- “The thermal camera does not lie, but you need to ask the right question.”
- “Asphalt vs white surface under the same sun.”
- “Emissivity: the small setting that can mislead a thermal image.”

### 4. LinkedIn, Medium and blog articles

Medium-form pieces can explain the method and the limits:

1. **Measure before arguing:** a thermal protocol for comparing materials under the sun.
2. **Why a thermal image needs metadata to become evidence.**
3. **GWP100, GTP and GWP\*:** useful metrics, real limits.
4. **Local CO2 depletion:** what it says and what it does not say about the global system.
5. **Urban materials, albedo and surface temperature.**
6. **A physical audit of climate assumptions:** toward a research agenda.

### 5. Press, interviews and presentations

Press and interviews should come later, once there is a clean pilot dataset, publishable visual material and clear caveats.

Two presentation tracks are planned:

- a **technical deck** for researchers, engineers and climate/data professionals;
- a **commercial deck** for companies, architects, facility managers, ESG teams and local administrations.

The commercial version should focus on what can be decided locally: materials, roofs, pavements, before/after comparisons and seasonal audits. It should also be explicit about what the method does not claim.

### 6. Critical IPCC angle

The strongest version of the IPCC-related angle is not “the IPCC is wrong”. It is more precise:

> climate metrics are tools, not substitutes for observed physics at every decision scale.

A responsible critique should:

- cite primary sources;
- separate scientific assessment from regulatory or media use;
- explain what the IPCC framework does well;
- show where a local decision needs additional metrics.

### 7. Long-form series, book or meta-study

If the evidence base grows, the project can become a 10-part series, a book outline or a scientific meta-study connecting:

- climate impact metrics;
- CO2 persistence and local depletion dynamics;
- surface material absorption;
- thermal imaging as a reproducible data method;
- decision-scale climate interpretation.

A possible working title for a future paper/meta-study:

> From global climate metrics to local thermal evidence: a methodological review of CO2 impact metrics, surface material absorption, and decision-scale climate interpretation.

## Publication rule

The editorial rule is:

> no high-commitment content should depend on evidence that has not passed through a lower-risk format first.

In practice:

```text
Raw data / source
  -> minimum validation
  -> internal note
  -> micro-post
  -> short video or graphic
  -> article
  -> presentation / press / interview
  -> long-form series / paper / book
```

That keeps the project ambitious without losing scientific discipline.

## Next practical step

After the first real thermal field session, the pipeline should generate:

- a clean CSV;
- a summary by material/sample;
- a temperature-vs-time chart;
- a delta-vs-white chart;
- a short technical report;
- three small X/Twitter posts;
- one short-video script;
- one LinkedIn/Medium draft.

The goal is not to publish more noise. The goal is to turn measurement into evidence, evidence into interpretation, and interpretation into useful decisions.
